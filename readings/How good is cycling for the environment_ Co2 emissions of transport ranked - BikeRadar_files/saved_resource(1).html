                                              It's worth keeping in mind that I've assumed every extra calorie burned is an extra calorie eaten, which is a worst-case scenario for human-powered activities. I think it's a reasonable assumption for cycling, but perhaps not for walking short trips.

I like how in Holland or Denmark, if you want to travel somewhere within 10km, you just get on your bike without thinking and go.

                                                  berndb brotherfranciz • 2 months ago • edited

                                                  That will depend greatly on your riding speed. Lycra, clipless etc are gimmicks when it comes to short-distance commuting. On a related note I've done the exercise, so to speak, to find out whether there would be a time saving on my 7km o/w commute by not changing into cycling clothes but instead riding slower but in office clothes (notwithstanding rainy days), avoiding changing time and dealing with sweat. It was surpisingly hard to determine which was better, which suggests that if the commute were shorter (e.g. 5km), the regular clothes/slower speed option would almost certainly win out, in my case anyway. Adding to that the carbon benefits of reducing bike specific gear would raise that distance further. Your mileage will vary of course, so do some experimenting to find out!

                                                                The only thing that bugs me in the environmental cost of the batteries, often overlooked when we only compute the Co2 emissions. To be as green as possible, it would mean making sure they are all recycled and possibly that standards for metal extraction improve. Sure, pollution would be lower in areas where such batteries are in use (better air quality), but this currently involves a massive soil pollution during extraction of lithium carbonate. It uses a lot of water in areas where it is in dire need, plus toxic waste leaking from the mines. Same for cobalt, one of the worst polluting metal during extraction.
                                                                Wired UK made an interesting article about it: ''The spiralling environmental cost of our lithium battery addiction''.
